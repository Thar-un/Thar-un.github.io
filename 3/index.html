Encrypted IDORs

A bug bounty story

This post describes a vulnerability I found and responsibly disclosed through a bug bounty program. All identifying details are intentionally removed.

I was scrolling through HackerOne notifications when I saw it. +2 reputation. If you're in bug bounty, you know what that means — a report you were tracking just got resolved. A fix went live somewhere.

Most people see +2 and move on. I opened Burp Suite.

I had filed a report on this target months ago — an image processing platform. My report got marked as a duplicate of someone else's finding, which stung. But now the original was resolved, meaning the engineering team shipped a patch.

The question that keeps me up at night after every +2: did they actually fix it, or did they just fix the specific reproduction steps?

• • •

The platform's image security works in two layers. Every photo gets a numeric ID when uploaded. But you can't use that raw ID to view the image. The platform encrypts it into a token first, and only the encrypted version works in the render URL. Think of it as a locked door — the numeric ID is the address, the encrypted token is the key.

The original bug was about getting keys for doors you don't own. The fix was supposed to stop that.

I fired up the same endpoint that was patched. Sent a request with someone else's asset ID in the body. My auth token. Their image ID.

The response came back with their encrypted token sitting right there in the JSON.

The door was still handing out keys to anyone who asked.

• • •

Pushing It

The first version of my report had a constraint — the victim needed to share at least one image with the attacker first. From a shared image's metadata, you could extract account identifiers and ID patterns to enumerate everything else.

But "requires victim interaction" is the kind of phrase that gets your severity dropped. I wanted zero interaction.

So I started studying the IDs.

The asset IDs are 16 digits long. Sounds like a lot. Except they're not random. A big chunk of the digits at the start and end are constant across users. That leaves roughly 6 digits in the middle that actually vary. A million combinations. My laptop can chew through that before lunch.

The account IDs? Same story. Numeric, pattern-based, with a small window of real entropy.

And the same endpoint that encrypts asset IDs will also encrypt account IDs if you feed them in. It doesn't validate what you give it. It just encrypts and returns.

So the full chain became:

  1. Brute-force the middle digits of asset IDs — collect encrypted tokens for every valid image
  2. Brute-force account IDs the same way — get encrypted account identifiers
  3. One more value needed for the render URL is common across all users
  4. Construct the render URL with all three encrypted values
  5. Follow the redirect. Private image loads.

I automated the whole thing. The final severity was High.

• • •

Three Things That Made This Work

The encryption endpoint had no ownership check. Its job was to convert numeric IDs into encrypted tokens. But it did this for any ID you asked about, not just your own. Imagine a locksmith who cuts keys for any address you give them, no questions asked.

Predictable numeric IDs. Sixteen digits sounds intimidating until you realise most of them are constant across users. Six digits of actual entropy is a million possibilities — minutes of automated requests, not years.

The render endpoint trusted the token blindly. Once you had the encrypted values, the image was served with no additional auth check. The entire security model rested on one assumption: "they can't get the encrypted token." And that assumption was broken.

• • •

What I Took Away

Don't accept the first severity rating. If you believe the impact is higher, demonstrate it. A working script pulling private data beats paragraphs about CVSS vectors.
